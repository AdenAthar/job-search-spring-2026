# Reflection

## Activity Description

The goal of this adventure was to run a real, documented job search targeting entry-level SWE and ML engineering roles. I set up a tracking system and systematically applied to 30+ positions between mid-January and late April 2026. Roles ranged from general software engineering to specialized ML and AI positions at companies like Cisco, Yahoo, LinkedIn, Palantir, Quora/Poe, Workday, Tata Consultancy Services, Capgemini, and various startups. The expectation I set for myself was to treat each application seriously — reading the job description, checking whether my background was a reasonable fit, and adjusting my materials where it made sense. The April push was more volume-oriented, using Handshake alongside direct portals to broaden reach as graduation approached.

## Technical and Professional Decisions

The biggest decision was how to split my time between SWE-focused roles and ML-focused ones. I ended up not committing to one track, which was intentional. My background is genuinely split — I've done web dev, systems work, and applied ML research — so narrowing too early felt like leaving options on the table. The tradeoff is that it's harder to craft a single tight narrative, so I maintained two resume versions and switched between them depending on the role.

Another decision was targeting a mix of company sizes and sectors. The April wave included defense contractors (NSWCDD), large consulting firms (Capgemini, TCS, Infinite Computer Solutions), tech companies (Palantir, Quora, Cohere, Workday), and staffing firms (NAMO Technologies). The logic: with OPT starting in July, I needed volume across sectors that sponsor visas, and some of the larger consulting and defense firms have strong sponsorship track records even if the roles aren't glamorous. Companies like Palantir and Quora were reach applications aligned with my ML background.

I also decided to include roles that weren't a perfect fit on paper — AI Training Specialist at Prolific, Data Analyst at Infinite Computer Solutions — because they either paid well or represented a foot-in-the-door at an organization I could grow within.

## Contributions

This was entirely independent work. I used my existing resume and cover letter as a base and iterated from there. I consulted a mentor from the WSU Boeing mentorship program about framing my ML research experience, and that feedback shaped how I described the Granny open-source project on my resume. All applications, research, and tracking were done solo.

## Quality Assessment

Volume is there — 30+ applications is meaningful output. But targeting could've been sharper earlier. Some of the January applications were scattershot while I was still figuring out what I wanted to prioritize. The April wave was more efficient but leaned heavily toward applying quickly rather than customizing deeply. If I repeated this, I'd spend more time upfront mapping out which roles genuinely excited me versus which ones I applied to just to hit a number. I'd also track response rates by role type from the start — by now it's clear ML-specific roles have a lower callback rate than general SWE roles, and knowing that earlier would have changed my strategy.

A few rejections — Advantest and Cambia Health — came back quickly, which suggests I wasn't a strong match on paper for those particular specs. That's useful signal.

## Career Alignment

This directly advanced my short-term goal of landing a full-time role post-graduation. Beyond the applications, the process forced me to articulate my skills in writing repeatedly, which made me better at explaining what I actually do. It also gave me a clearer picture of what the entry-level ML market looks like for new grads right now — competitive and noisy, with most traction coming from companies that already value computer vision or applied ML backgrounds. The Palantir and Quora applications in particular feel well-aligned with where I want to go, and getting those acknowledgements is encouraging even if outcomes are uncertain.
