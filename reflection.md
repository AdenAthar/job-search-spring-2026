# Reflection

## What I Did

The goal of this adventure was to run a real, documented job search targeting entry-level SWE and ML engineering roles. I set up a tracking spreadsheet and systematically applied to over 20 positions between mid-January and the end of the assignment window. Roles ranged from general software engineering to more specialized ML and AI positions at companies like Cisco, Yahoo, LinkedIn, Epson, and a handful of startups. The expectation I set for myself was to treat each application seriously — not just mass-applying, but actually reading the job description, checking whether my background was a reasonable fit, and adjusting my resume or cover letter where it made sense.

## Decisions I Made

The biggest decision was how to split my time between SWE-focused roles and ML-focused ones. I ended up not committing to one track, which was intentional. My background is genuinely split — I've done web dev, systems work, and applied ML research — so narrowing down too early felt like leaving options on the table. The tradeoff is that it's harder to craft a single tight narrative, so I ended up maintaining two versions of my resume and switching between them depending on the role.

Another decision was targeting a mix of company sizes. I applied to large companies like Cisco and Yahoo alongside smaller startups. The thinking was that large companies offer structure and credibility early in your career, but smaller companies often give you more responsibility faster, which I think matters a lot for long-term growth in ML.

## How I Worked

This was mostly independent work. I used my existing resume and cover letter as a base and iterated from there. I also talked to a few people in my network, including a mentor from the Boeing mentorship program I'm part of, about how to frame my ML research experience for industry roles. That feedback shaped how I described the open-source project I contributed to on my resume.

## How I'd Assess My Performance

Honestly, the volume is there but the targeting could've been sharper earlier on. Some of the January applications were a bit scattershot while I was still figuring out what I actually wanted to prioritize. If I were doing this again, I'd spend more time upfront mapping out which roles genuinely excited me versus which ones I applied to just to hit a number. I'd also start networking earlier in parallel with applications rather than treating it as an afterthought.

## Career Alignment

This directly advanced my short-term goal of landing a full-time role after graduation. Beyond just the applications themselves, the process forced me to articulate my skills in writing over and over, which made me better at explaining what I actually do. It also surfaced a clearer picture of what the market looks like for new grads in ML right now, which is useful context regardless of how individual applications go.
